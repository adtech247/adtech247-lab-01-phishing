## Email 1 — 🔴 PHISHING

**The tells:**

- **Fake sender domain:** `paypa1-secure.com` — that's a **number 1** replacing the "l" in PayPal. Classic typosquatting.
- **Urgency + fear:** "within 24 HOURS or permanently suspended" — designed to make you panic and act without thinking.
- **Suspicious link:** `account-secure.ru` — a Russian domain (.ru) that has nothing to do with PayPal.
- **Generic greeting:** "Dear Valued Customer" instead of your name.
- **Threatening tone:** Real companies don't threaten permanent closure over "unusual activity."

**Verdict:** Textbook phishing. Every red flag in the book.

---

## Email 2 — 🟢 LEGITIMATE

**Why it's safe:**

- **Sender domain matches:** `@yourcompany.com` — an internal colleague.
- **Contextual and specific:** References a real meeting, specific slides, a clear deadline.
- **No urgency or pressure:** Normal business request with a reasonable timeline.
- **No credential requests:** Just asking for feedback.
- **Expected attachment:** A .pptx deck that fits the context of the message.

**Verdict:** This is a normal work email. Safe.

> ⚠️ **Note:** Even legit-looking internal emails *can* be spoofed. If an attachment or request ever feels off, verify with the sender through another channel (Slack, phone). But nothing here raises a flag.

## Email 3 — 🔴 PHISHING

**The tells:**

- **Fake domain:** `microsoft-support.com` is NOT a real Microsoft domain. Microsoft uses `microsoft.com`.
- **Link mismatch:** `outlook-webmail-verify.com` — not an official Microsoft/Outlook URL.
- **Credential harvesting:** Asks you to "enter your username and current password." **This is the biggest red flag of all** — legitimate IT will NEVER ask for your password.
- **Urgency:** "expires in 2 days" pressure tactic.

**Verdict:** Credential-harvesting phishing. The password request alone is a dead giveaway.

---

## Email 4 — 🟢 LEGITIMATE

**Why it's safe:**

- **Real domain:** `no-reply@amazon.com` — the actual Amazon domain.
- **Personalized:** Uses your real name (Augusto).
- **Specific and expected:** Real order number, real product, real delivery date.
- **No suspicious links:** Directs you to check "Your Orders" on Amazon directly — doesn't include a sketchy click-here link.
- **No credential requests or urgency.**

**Verdict:** A normal shipping notification. Safe.

> 💡 **Pro tip:** Even here, the safest move is to never click email links — just open the Amazon app or type amazon.com yourself. But this email itself is legitimate.

---

## Email 5 — 🔴 PHISHING

**The tells:**

- **Suspicious domain:** `your-company-benefits.info` — the `.info` domain and odd naming don't match a real internal HR address.
- **Requests banking details AND password by email:** No legitimate HR department will EVER ask for your account number, routing number, and banking password via email reply.
- **Urgency:** "must be completed today to avoid a delay in your paycheck" — weaponizing your fear of not getting paid.
- **Generic greeting:** "Dear Employee."
- **Reply-with-sensitive-data:** Legit systems use secure portals, never email replies for financial info.

**Verdict:** Dangerous phishing — a business email compromise (BEC) attempt targeting payroll. This type costs companies billions per year.

## The Two Golden Rules

If you remember nothing else from this lab, remember these:

1. **No legitimate company will ever ask for your password by email.** Ever. Full stop.
2. **When in doubt, don't click — verify through another channel.** Type the URL yourself or call the sender.
